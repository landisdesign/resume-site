What I offer

The other areas of this site illustrate and describe my technical abilities, but don’t give a sense of what I bring to a team beyond the technical.

Rapid learning skills

I’ve been creating web pages since 1997. I fell into that when my staffing partner at Aquent said, “I have a job that requires web skills as well as your QuarkXpress knowledge. Do you think you can do both?” I responded, “I can learn!” and spent a week learning about HTML before going onto the job. I haven’t looked back since.

When faced with a new technology, I absorb it and put it to use quickly. Typically I go from newbie to expert in that technology before project development is completed. When I worked on the AEM conversion for americanfunds.com, I started with experience from a home-grown, REST-less, file-system-based CMS. Within three months I was bringing valuable contributions to architectural discussions with our consultants.

After leaving a technologically conservative firm and being away from technology for over five years, I’m seeing techniques and technologies that excite me. In the process of building this site I learned how use Bootstrap 4’s layout and utilities, SCSS, CSS flex layout, and BEM design, all within 10 days. It took me less than a week to learn about React and Google’s search APIs and finish the application on this site, and 6 days to do the same with Angular.

I recognize that, after my time away, I will need to catch up. I posit that it will take far less time for me to do so than might be anticipated.

I look forward to learning with each new project I face.

Communication skills

In one of my early performance reviews, my manager mentioned that I had the ability to explain technical issues to non-technical people – “managers, team-members, clients, ice-cream vendors.”

As facetious as that was, it highlights my ability to translate ideas across fields. I understand and speak fluently the languages of visual design, writing, and business as well as technology. The ability to explain technical constraints to designers in a way that helps them understand and accept the constraints’ purposes, not just their presence, has been invaluable.

I appreciate that each group has concerns and needs as valid as the others’, and work to bring understanding to each group. I enjoy playing a BSA role, helping designers and business representatives clarify their requirements while working with fellow developers to find ways to fulfill challenging requests.

In addition to facilitating the design process, I’m also comfortable managing timeline and resource expectations. I believe there is no such thing as bad news, only valuable information. If I see something beyond my purview that raises concerns, whether in timelines or technical decisions, I will bring it to the appropriate level of attention, so that those whose purview it is can make their decisions with all the information, not just information I think they want to hear. Once a decision is made, I will implement it to the best of my ability.

Attention to detail

In his seminal book Code Complete, Steve McConnell makes the claim that a developer’s first and foremost job is to manage complexity. I take this to heart, aiming to make my code as readable and self-explanatory as possible. Beyond commenting, I break up longer methods and use human-readable names and liberal white space, to help make the code readable and easier for the developers after me to understand what I’ve done.
